How's Your Mind?

Why Mental Health Matters

Everyone experiences mental health along a spectrum, much like everyone experiences physical health from wellness to illness. Mental illnesses affect one's mood, thinking, emotions, or behavior and get in the way of someone's ability to live, laugh, love, or learn. Nearly half of all people will experience mental illness at some point in their life. Suicide is the 10th leading cause of death in the US, and the second leading cause of death among young people, and yet many - if not most- people still feel uncomfortable speaking about their own or another's mental health.

Let's Chat

Mental health isn't just about mental illness - it also encompasses mental wellness. By normalizing conversations about our minds, we can make a conversation about mood disorders or mental health crises as commonplace as those about a cold, appendicitis, or a heart attack. We know if someone is choking we need to perform the Heimlich, or if someone goes into cardiac arrest we need to perform CPR, but most people don't know what to do if someone in their life is suicidal. By normalizing talking about mental health, we can not only better know ourselves and preventative measures we can take to be healthy, we will be better prepared to help ourselves and others when mental illnesses arise.
